## 1. COMPANY OVERVIEW

**ITC Limited is India's most unique conglomerate: a business that generates exceptional returns from one of the world's most regulated industries and systematically deploys that capital to build the consumer goods company of the next generation.**

ITC Limited (NSE: ITC) is a diversified Indian conglomerate with principal operations across five verticals: FMCG-Cigarettes, FMCG-Others (packaged foods and consumer goods), Hotels (now partially demerged), Paperboards/Paper/Packaging, and Agribusiness. Headquartered in Kolkata and incorporated in 1910, ITC has evolved from a pure tobacco company into a multi-sector enterprise with a consolidated revenue of approximately ₹73,465 crore for FY25, making it one of India's largest consumer-facing corporates by gross revenues.

### Revenue Mix and Profit Contribution

The revenue and profit architecture of ITC is deeply asymmetric — a feature that defines both its investment thesis and its valuation complexity. For FY25:

- **FMCG-Cigarettes**: Contributed approximately ₹32,631 crore in gross revenues (~44% of total), but delivered segment EBIT of ₹21,091 crore, representing roughly 78% of total segment EBIT. EBIT margins in the cigarettes business run at approximately 58–65% on net segment revenues, making it among the highest-margin legal consumer businesses globally.
- **FMCG-Others**: Contributed gross revenues of approximately ₹21,982 crore (~30% of total). This segment, which encompasses packaged foods, personal care, stationery, and other branded consumer goods, crossed breakeven in FY23 after years of brand-building investment and has been generating modest but growing positive EBIT since. The consumer spend on ITC's FMCG products has reached approximately ₹34,000 crore in FY25 (including trade channel markups), indicating strong underlying demand.
- **Agribusiness**: Contributed approximately ₹19,753 crore in revenues (~27% of total), driven by robust growth in leaf tobacco exports and value-added agri products. This segment generates more modest absolute EBIT as it is a commodity-facing B2B business.
- **Paperboards, Paper & Packaging**: Contributed approximately ₹8,000–8,500 crore in revenues (~11–12% of total). The segment faced significant headwinds in FY25 due to low-priced Chinese and Indonesian paper imports flooding domestic markets and unprecedented increases in wood/pulp prices, with operating profit declining approximately 34% year-on-year.
- **Hotels (discontinued operations)**: The hotels business was demerged effective January 1, 2025, with ITC Hotels Limited listing on January 29, 2025. ITC Hotels reported standalone revenue of ₹3,333 crore for FY25 (full year, pre-demerger included in discontinued operations). ITC retains a 40% equity stake in the demerged entity.

The aggregate EBITDA for FY25 from continuing operations stood at ₹24,025 crore with an EBITDA margin of 34.7%, reflecting the extraordinary profitability of the cigarettes segment.

### Geographic Presence

ITC's business is overwhelmingly India-focused, with domestic revenues constituting over 85% of total revenues. The primary export-oriented business is agribusiness, which exports leaf tobacco to global buyers (particularly from the United States, Europe, and Southeast Asia), wheat, spices, coffee, and other agricultural commodities. ITC Infotech, the group's IT services arm, derives a significant portion of its revenues from international markets, particularly the US and UK, though it remains a smaller segment. Paperboards exports form a modest third revenue stream internationally.

### Scale and Key Metrics

As of March 12, 2026, ITC's market capitalization stands at approximately ₹3,87,153 crore (approximately USD 45 billion). The stock trades at ₹306.80, having declined from its 52-week high of ₹444.20, partly reflecting the sharp cigarette excise duty hike imposed by the Central Excise (Amendment) Act 2025, effective January 1, 2026. The company employs approximately 26,000–30,000 people across its businesses.

Key financial metrics for FY25:
- Revenue (gross): ₹73,465 crore
- EBITDA (continuing operations): ₹24,025 crore; EBITDA margin: 34.7%
- Adjusted PAT (continuing operations): ₹19,701 crore
- Total PAT (including discontinued operations gain): ₹35,196 crore (includes ₹15,163 crore exceptional gain from Hotels demerger)
- ROCE: 36.8% | ROE: 27.3%
- Net cash position: approximately ₹18,000–20,000 crore (net of debt of ₹363 crore against cash of ₹18,560 crore)
- Total dividend FY25: ₹14.35 per share (interim ₹6.50 + final ₹7.85)
- Dividend yield: approximately 4.64% at current market price
- Dividend payout ratio: approximately 78–80% of adjusted PAT

### The Conglomerate Discount Debate

ITC has been a persistent subject of the conglomerate discount debate in Indian equity markets. The argument is straightforward: a sum-of-parts valuation of ITC's cigarette business alone — valued at a premium multiple reflecting its oligopolistic market position and cash-flow durability — would imply a value per share higher than where ITC currently trades when the other businesses (some of which destroy capital on a standalone basis) are added.

The counter-argument, which ITC's management has articulated through the "ITC Next" strategy, is that the cross-subsidization model is deliberate: the extraordinarily high free cash flows generated by the cigarettes business (EBIT margins of 58–65%, minimal capex requirements) fund the brand investments needed to build next-generation FMCG franchises that would be prohibitively expensive to establish on standalone capital. The FMCG-Others segment has now crossed EBIT breakeven and is growing at a healthy clip, validating this thesis partially.

The partial demerger of the hotels business (effective January 2025) represents management's acknowledgment that discrete unlocking of value can be achieved by separating businesses with different capital structures, growth profiles, and investor bases.

### Why ITC Is Structurally Unique

No other Indian company has assembled a combination of: (1) a domestic oligopoly in a heavily regulated, sin-taxed consumer product that generates 60%+ EBIT margins; (2) a nascent but growing FMCG portfolio funded entirely from internal accruals; (3) one of India's largest rural internet infrastructure networks (e-Choupal); (4) an integrated paperboards-to-packaging value chain servicing its own FMCG business; and (5) a luxury hospitality brand with pan-India presence. This combination creates complexity but also resilience: when one segment faces headwinds (as paperboards did in FY25), the cigarettes cash engine absorbs the impact without compromising investment in growth businesses.

## 2. COMPANY HISTORY

**ITC's 115-year journey from a British colonial tobacco company to an Indian conglomerate aspiring to FMCG leadership is one of the most remarkable corporate transformations in Indian business history.**

### Founding and Colonial Origins (1910–1947)

ITC Limited was established on August 24, 1910, as the Imperial Tobacco Company of India Limited, registered in Kolkata as a subsidiary of the British-American Tobacco Company (BAT). The company's genesis was rooted in the colonial tobacco trade: the firm succeeded W.D. & H.O. Wills's Indian operations to manufacture and distribute cigarettes to a growing Indian market.

In its earliest years, ITC focused on building the agricultural supply chain that tobacco manufacturing demands. In 1911, the company entered into partnerships with farmers in southern India to source leaf tobacco. The Indian Leaf Tobacco Development Company Limited was formed in the Guntur district of Andhra Pradesh in 1912, establishing what would become ITC's deep agri roots. The first cigarette factory was established in Bangalore in 1913. By 1926, the company had acquired its landmark Kolkata property at 37, Chowringhee (now Jawaharlal Nehru Road), and by 1928, construction had commenced on 'Virginia House' — the Kolkata headquarters that remains in use today.

### Indianisation and Independence Era (1947–1970)

The post-Independence political economy of India necessitated a progressive Indianisation of British-owned companies. ITC was converted into a Public Limited Company on October 27, 1954, with an initial Indian shareholding of 6%. The pivotal moment came in 1969, when Ajit Narain Haskar became the first Indian Chairman of ITC, marking the formal transition from British to Indian executive leadership.

Reflecting this transformation, the company's name was changed from "Imperial Tobacco Company of India Limited" to "India Tobacco Company Limited" in 1970, and subsequently to "I.T.C. Limited" in 1974 — deliberately stripping the tobacco-centric nomenclature to signal diversification intent. The full stop separated name became simply "ITC Limited" by the 2000s, cementing a brand identity no longer anchored to tobacco.

### Strategic Diversification: Hotels and Agribusiness (1970s–2000s)

The most consequential strategic decision of ITC's post-Independence history was the entry into the hotels business in the mid-1970s. ITC Hotels Limited was incorporated, and the company opened its first luxury hotel in Chennai (then Madras) in 1975. The strategic rationale was a classic conglomerate hedge: tobacco revenues were under increasing social scrutiny globally, and the hotels business offered a high-capital, high-prestige avenue for deploying surpluses. The ITC Windsor in Bangalore (1983) and the iconic ITC Maurya in New Delhi followed, cementing ITC's credentials in the luxury segment.

The agribusiness deepening accelerated through the 1990s as ITC expanded its leaf tobacco procurement network across Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, and other states. But the truly transformative initiative came in June 2000 with the launch of e-Choupal — what would become the world's largest internet-based rural intervention. Conceived and executed under S. Sivakumar, e-Choupal deployed internet kiosks in rural villages to provide farmers with real-time price information, weather data, and agronomic guidance — bypassing exploitative middlemen and creating direct procurement channels for ITC. The network grew to cover over 35,000 villages and has been a landmark case study at Harvard Business School and INSEAD.

ITC entered the paperboards business in 1979 through Bhadrachalam Paperboards, recognising that vertical integration into packaging could reduce supply chain vulnerability for its cigarettes business and create a scalable standalone enterprise.

### The FMCG Pivot (2000–2019)

The early 2000s saw ITC make the most ambitious bet in its history: the systematic entry into consumer goods categories to build an FMCG business that could eventually reduce dependence on cigarettes. The company launched Aashirvaad atta (wheat flour) in 2002, Sunfeast biscuits in 2003, and Bingo! snack foods in 2007. These were not opportunistic forays but a coordinated strategy to leverage ITC's existing distribution infrastructure (built for cigarettes), its Agribusiness procurement network (direct access to wheat and other commodities), and its Paperboards division (in-house packaging supply). Each launch was underpinned by the cigarette cash engine.

The FMCG-Others business initially incurred significant losses as brand-building investments were heavy and scale took time to build. For more than a decade, the segment was EBIT-negative, drawing criticism from investors who argued that capital should be returned rather than deployed in loss-making consumer goods ventures. This argument was at the core of the conglomerate discount debate.

### BAT's Evolving Stake

British American Tobacco's stake in ITC has been a defining structural feature of the company's ownership. Through most of its history, BAT held approximately 29–32% of ITC's equity, making it the largest single shareholder and entitling it to board representation and certain veto rights in shareholder agreements. The relationship was broadly cooperative, providing ITC access to BAT's global tobacco technology and leaf procurement expertise while BAT benefited from ITC's extraordinary Indian cigarette economics.

However, beginning in 2024, BAT began actively reducing its ITC stake, selling 3.5% in March 2024 (bringing its holding to approximately 25.44%) and a further 2.5% in May 2025 (bringing its holding to approximately 22.9%) and in doing so, relinquishing its formal veto power in ITC's shareholder agreements. BAT separately sold most of its ITC Hotels shares received upon demerger, retaining approximately 6.3% of ITC Hotels after a December 2025 block trade. The reasons for BAT's partial exit are complex: BAT faces its own pressures as a global tobacco company managing a declining cigarettes portfolio globally while investing in next-generation products; the ITC stake monetisation provides capital for BAT's $2 billion share buyback program. Critically, BAT remains ITC's single largest shareholder and the strategic relationship in tobacco continues.

### ITC Next and the Hotels Demerger (2019–Present)

Sanjiv Puri, who became Chairman & MD in May 2019, articulated the "ITC Next" strategy — a comprehensive reset of the company's ambitions toward becoming a consumer-centric, technology-enabled, climate-positive enterprise. The strategy has three pillars: accelerated FMCG growth (100+ new product launches per year), digital and agritech investment (e-Choupal 4.0), and structural simplification (including the Hotels demerger).

The Hotels demerger was announced in September 2023, received NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal, Kolkata bench) approval on October 4, 2024, and became effective on January 1, 2025. ITC Hotels Limited listed on NSE/BSE on January 29, 2025, with shareholders receiving one ITC Hotels share for every ten ITC shares held. ITC retained a 40% strategic stake in the entity. The demerger crystallised a portion of hidden value and allowed ITC Hotels to pursue its own capital allocation and growth strategy independently — targeting 220 properties by 2030.

## 3. MANAGEMENT TEAM

**ITC's management depth is exceptional by Indian corporate standards: a cadre of career executives who have grown entirely within the ITC system, combining institutional knowledge with the operational breadth demanded by a five-segment conglomerate.**

### Sanjiv Puri — Chairman & Managing Director

Sanjiv Puri is among the most accomplished corporate leaders in India today, a recognition formalised through his receipt of the Best CEO Award in the FMCG category at Fortune India's Best CEOs 2025 and the AIMA-JRD Tata Corporate Leadership Award.

Born in 1963, Puri is a graduate of IIT Kanpur (widely regarded as India's most selective engineering institution) and holds a postgraduate degree from the Wharton School of Business at the University of Pennsylvania. He joined ITC in January 1986, beginning a four-decade career within a single company — a rarity in the modern era of executive mobility.

His career trajectory at ITC has been notable for its breadth. He spent the early years in operational roles across ITC's businesses, gaining exposure to both the core tobacco franchise and emerging diversifications. He was appointed Managing Director of ITC Infotech India Limited in May 2006, where he led the IT services business during a formative period of its globalisation. He then moved to Nepal as Managing Director of Surya Nepal Private Limited (ITC's associate company) from October 2001 to April 2006, gaining international management experience in a regulated tobacco environment. He returned to take charge of the core Tobacco Division as Divisional Chief Executive in December 2009, overseeing the cigarettes business through a period of steady volume growth and pricing excellence.

Puri was elevated to President of FMCG Businesses in December 2014, taking ownership of the largest strategic bet of ITC's transformation: building a credible FMCG challenger. He served as Chief Operating Officer from July 2016 to February 2017, then CEO from February 2017, Managing Director from May 2018, and Chairman & Managing Director from May 13, 2019.

As Chairman & MD, Puri has presided over three landmark outcomes: (1) the FMCG-Others business crossing EBIT profitability in FY23 after years of sustained investment, validating the strategic thesis; (2) the structurally significant Hotels demerger, addressing a persistent investor ask for simplification; and (3) maintaining cigarettes volume and pricing resilience in an increasingly adverse regulatory environment. His ₹20,000 crore medium-term manufacturing expansion plan announced at ITC's 2025 AGM signals continued confidence in India's consumption growth story.

Beyond ITC, Puri serves as Immediate Past President of the Confederation of Indian Industry (CII), Chairman of the Board of Governors of IIT Gandhinagar, and is a member of the BRICS Business Council and the National Council of Applied Economic Research. He holds an Honorary Doctorate from XIM University, Bhubaneshwar.

### S. Sivakumar — Group Head, Agri & IT Businesses

S. Sivakumar is one of India's foremost practitioners of agribusiness innovation and rural development, a distinction earned through three decades of building ITC's agricultural supply chain and inventing one of the most celebrated rural technology interventions in global development literature.

Sivakumar graduated as the class topper of the Institute of Rural Management, Anand (IRMA) — India's premier rural management institution — in 1983. Before joining ITC, he spent six years with a farmers' cooperative, grounding his career in the realities of smallholder agriculture in India. He joined ITC in 1989 and has spent his entire corporate career in the agribusiness domain.

As Divisional Chief Executive of ITC's Agri Business Division from 1996 to 2017, Sivakumar was the principal architect and executor of e-Choupal, launched in June 2000. The initiative placed internet kiosks in rural villages, providing farmers with real-time price data, weather forecasts, and agronomic information — eliminating the informational asymmetry that middlemen had exploited for decades. E-Choupal has been the subject of case studies at Harvard Business School and INSEAD, and is frequently cited in global development discussions as a template for private-sector rural development done well. Under Sivakumar's leadership, e-Choupal expanded to cover over 35,000 villages across 10 states, with the 4.0 version targeting 70,000 villages and integrating agri-services aggregation.

In his current role as Group Head of Agri & IT Businesses, Sivakumar oversees both the Agri Business Division and ITC Infotech — an unusual portfolio that reflects his comfort with technology as an enabler of business outcomes. He is Vice Chairman of ITC Infotech India Limited and Chairman of Technico Agri Sciences Limited. He serves on the Advisory Council to the Ministry of Rural Development for the National Rural Livelihoods Mission, on the Commodity Derivatives Advisory Committee of SEBI, and on the Management Committee of the National Agricultural Higher Education Project of the Ministry of Agriculture.

The Agri Business segment under his stewardship delivered revenue growth of approximately 25% in FY25, driven by strong leaf tobacco export demand and expanding value-added agri product exports.

### Hemant Malik — Wholetime Director, Foods Business Division

Hemant Malik represents the commercial leadership backbone of ITC's FMCG transformation. An MBA graduate from IIM Kolkata — India's oldest and one of its most prestigious management institutions — Malik joined ITC in 1989, the same year as Sivakumar, beginning a career that has spanned the full arc of ITC's consumer goods ambition.

Malik's career at ITC has been defined by progression through the company's core commercial functions. He worked across the Tobacco division, Lifestyle Retailing, and Packaged Foods in his early years, building broad-based FMCG commercial experience. He was elevated to COO of Cigarette Brands and Supply Chain within the India Tobacco Division in August 2012, giving him oversight of the most complex brand portfolio management challenge in Indian consumer goods: maintaining market share and pricing power in a heavily regulated, oligopolistic category. He became Divisional Chief Executive of the India Tobacco Division in August 2015.

In October 2016, Malik transitioned to the Foods Business Division as its Divisional Chief Executive, taking charge of ITC's portfolio of packaged food brands at a critical juncture when scale was being built but profitability remained elusive. Under his leadership, the Foods business achieved significant milestones: Aashirvaad atta crossed ₹7,000 crore in annual sales to become India's leading branded atta; Sunfeast consolidated its position as a top-two biscuit brand; Bingo! became a major player in the snacks category; and the overall FMCG-Others business crossed EBIT breakeven. Malik was appointed as Wholetime Director on ITC's Board effective August 12, 2023, acknowledging the strategic centrality of the FMCG transformation to ITC's future.

His mandate going forward is to accelerate the ₹30,000+ crore revenue aspiration for FMCG-Others by FY28 while improving profitability margins, managing the growing complexity of the portfolio (100+ new launches annually), and navigating the competitive intensity from HUL, Britannia, and Nestle.

### Nakul Anand — Formerly Executive Director, Hotels Division

Nakul Anand's career at ITC spans four-plus decades, nearly as long as the company's Hotels business has existed. An Economics Honours graduate from Delhi University with an Advanced Management Programme degree from Bond University, Australia, Anand joined erstwhile ITC Hotels Limited as a Management Trainee in 1978 — one year before the Bhadrachalam paperboards venture was established.

Anand rose through the hospitality ranks to become Managing Director of ITC Hotels (2003–2005) and subsequently served as Executive Director on ITC's main Board from January 3, 2011, with responsibility for the Hospitality and Travel & Tourism businesses. Over his tenure, he oversaw the expansion of ITC's hotel portfolio across multiple brands — ITC Hotels, WelcomHotel, Fortune, Storii, Mementos, and WelcomHeritage — and the repositioning of ITC's flagship properties as some of India's most awarded luxury hotels. Under his leadership, ITC Hotels built a reputation for culinary excellence, sustainability credentials (including India's first LEED Platinum certified hotel), and MICE (meetings, incentives, conferences, exhibitions) capabilities.

The Hotels demerger, which Anand's work helped set up structurally, was announced in September 2023 and completed in January 2025. Following the demerger and his retirement from ITC, Anand has joined Tricone Luxury Hotels as Chairman of its Advisory Board, taking his deep hospitality expertise into the broader industry. The demerged ITC Hotels entity now operates independently with its own board and management team, with Puri-led ITC retaining a 40% stake as a strategic anchor shareholder.

### Board Composition and Governance

ITC's Board includes BAT nominee directors, reflecting the British American Tobacco Company's position as the largest single shareholder (22.9% as of post-May 2025 stake reduction). The presence of BAT nominees provides ITC with access to global tobacco expertise but also creates periodic governance questions around alignment of interests, particularly as BAT's strategic focus has shifted toward next-generation tobacco products.

The Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC) is ITC's second-largest institutional shareholder with approximately 16% equity stake, providing a stable institutional anchor and, implicitly, a counterweight to any unilateral action by BAT. The absence of a traditional promoter group — no family or single controlling entity holds a majority — is a distinctive governance feature of ITC that distinguishes it from most large Indian companies. This structure, while providing professional management independence, has historically made it harder to implement structural changes swiftly.

## 4. PRODUCTS & SERVICES

**ITC's product portfolio spans five verticals, united by the common thread of distribution scale, commodity procurement expertise, and packaging self-sufficiency.**

### 1. FMCG-Cigarettes

Cigarettes remain ITC's defining business — financially, strategically, and historically. ITC commands approximately 75–77% of India's legal cigarettes market by volume, a position that has been remarkably stable over decades. The brand portfolio is comprehensive:

- **Gold Flake** (including Gold Flake Kings, Gold Flake Premium): India's largest-selling cigarette brand by volume; spans the premium to mid-premium segments
- **Classic** (including Classic Milds, Classic Regular): premium positioning, popular with urban consumers
- **Wills** (including Wills Navy Cut): heritage brand with strong urban loyalty
- **Bristol**: value segment, maintaining ITC's presence across price points
- **Flake**: additional variants targeting specific demographics

The economics of ITC's cigarettes business are extraordinary by any standard. The segment generated approximately ₹32,631 crore in gross revenues for FY25, with segment EBIT of approximately ₹21,091 crore — implying EBIT margins in the range of 58–65% on net revenues. The business requires minimal capital reinvestment (manufacturing assets are fully depreciated and the product is non-perishable), generates strong free cash flow, and has demonstrated semi-annual price increase capability — passing on excise duty hikes to consumers with limited volume impact, given the addictive nature of the product and ITC's pricing power in a duopoly.

The competitive structure is a functional duopoly: ITC (~75–77% share), Godfrey Phillips India (~10% share, affiliated with Philip Morris), and VST Industries (~9% share, affiliated with British American Tobacco's southern markets historically). This concentration means ITC sets the reference price point; any price hike is typically followed by competitors.

However, the regulatory environment is tightening substantially. The Central Excise (Amendment) Act 2025, effective January 1, 2026, dramatically increased specific excise duties on cigarettes from ₹200–735 per thousand sticks to ₹2,700–11,000 per thousand sticks — an increase of 10x–15x. The Tobacco Institute of India has warned that this will significantly expand the illicit cigarette market, which already accounts for approximately 26% of total cigarette consumption in India. Smuggled, counterfeit, and bidi substitution represent meaningful volume leakage risks for the legal industry. ITC's stock declined approximately 10% on the announcement.

### 2. FMCG-Others

The FMCG-Others business, with revenues of approximately ₹21,982 crore in FY25 and consumer spending of approximately ₹34,000 crore, is ITC's growth engine and the clearest demonstration of its long-term diversification thesis. The portfolio spans multiple categories:

**Staples and Foods (~75% of FMCG-Others)**
- **Aashirvaad**: India's largest branded atta (wheat flour) with over ₹7,000 crore in annual sales; also includes spices, salt, instant mixes, dairy, and poha. Aashirvaad has built a dominant position in branded staples by leveraging ITC's direct farmer procurement (via e-Choupal) to ensure quality and consistency
- **Sunfeast**: Biscuits, cookies, pasta, noodles. One of India's top-two biscuit brands, competing directly with Britannia across multiple sub-categories. The Marie Light and Farmlite variants have strong urban penetration
- **Yippee!**: Noodles brand positioned as the primary challenger to Nestle's Maggi, the dominant player in Indian instant noodles. Yippee! has taken share post the 2015 Maggi recall controversy
- **Bingo!**: Salted snacks and chips, competing with Frito-Lay (PepsiCo) and local players. Bingo! has been a genuine market-share gainer in a high-growth category
- **B Natural**: Fruit juices and beverages; positioning in the natural/no-concentrate segment

**Personal Care and Other**
- **Engage**: Deodorants and fragrances, competing in the premium mass market segment
- **Fiama**: Shower gels and soaps
- **Vivel**: Value soaps and shampoos
- **Classmate**: Notebooks, stationery, and art supplies; the largest stationery brand in India by volume

The segment's EBIT crossed positive territory in FY23 after nearly two decades of investment, a milestone that validates the fundamental premise of ITC's FMCG strategy. FY25 EBIT margins for FMCG-Others are approximately 9–10%, still well below mature FMCG peers but on an improving trajectory.

### 3. Hotels (Now ITC Hotels Limited — Demerged)

The Hotels business, now operating as an independently listed entity (ITC Hotels Limited) since January 29, 2025, had revenues of ₹3,333 crore for FY25 (a 44% surge year-over-year, reflecting post-COVID normalisation and the consolidation of several managed properties). PAT stood at ₹698 crore.

ITC Hotels operates over 140 properties with approximately 13,300 rooms under six brands:
- **ITC Hotels**: Flagship luxury properties (ITC Maurya, ITC Grand Chola, ITC Maratha, etc.); among India's most recognised luxury hotel brands; known for LEED Platinum green building certifications
- **Mementos**: Newer luxury brand for distinctively positioned properties
- **WelcomHotel**: Upper-upscale segment; 25+ properties across India
- **Storii**: Boutique premium positioning
- **Fortune**: Mid-market to upscale; the largest by property count; strong in Tier 2/3 cities
- **WelcomHeritage**: Heritage properties in palaces and heritage structures

ITC retains a 40% strategic stake in ITC Hotels Limited post-demerger, ensuring continued alignment while allowing the entity to pursue its own capital markets journey.

### 4. Paperboards, Paper & Packaging

ITC Bhadrachalam (ITC's Paperboards and Specialty Papers Division, or ITC-PSPD) is one of India's largest and most technologically sophisticated integrated paper mills, located in Bhadradri Kothagudem district of Telangana on the banks of the Godavari River. The facility has a production capacity of approximately 677,000 tonnes per annum of paperboard and paper.

The division produces value-added paperboards for consumer goods packaging, specialty papers for printing and writing, and coated paperboards used in food, beverage, and pharmaceutical packaging. Critically, it supplies a significant portion of ITC's own FMCG and cigarettes packaging requirements — an integrated supply chain advantage that reduces ITC's exposure to third-party packaging price volatility.

FY25 was a difficult year for this segment: revenues grew approximately 1% while operating profit declined approximately 34%, impacted by a combination of low-priced Chinese and Indonesian paper imports flooding domestic markets and an unprecedented rise in wood (raw material) prices. The segment is expected to recover as the government addresses dumping concerns and domestic demand recovers.

### 5. Agribusiness

ITC's Agribusiness Division is organised around two complementary activities: the procurement, processing, and export of agricultural commodities (leaf tobacco, wheat, spices, coffee, shrimp); and the e-Choupal rural platform.

The Agri Business segment posted revenues of approximately ₹19,753 crore in FY25, up 25% year-on-year, driven by strong international demand for Indian leaf tobacco and agri commodities. ITC is one of India's largest leaf tobacco exporters, with the segment participating in a global export market estimated at over USD 1 billion annually for Indian leaf.

The e-Choupal network, currently covering over 35,000 villages across 10 states through approximately 6,100 kiosks, is in transition to its 4.0 iteration — which envisions the platform as an aggregator of agri-services (seeds, inputs, credit, insurance, market linkages) across 70,000 villages, deepening ITC's role in rural India's agricultural economy.

## 5. CUSTOMERS & GO-TO-MARKET

**ITC's most durable competitive advantage in the consumer businesses is not brand equity alone — it is the proprietary distribution network that reaches approximately two million retail points without reliance on third-party distributors, a legacy asset built over decades in the cigarettes business.**

### Cigarettes Distribution

The cigarettes business is served through one of India's most extensive direct distribution networks, reaching approximately 2 million retail outlets across urban, semi-urban, and rural India. Given the restrictions on advertising tobacco products under the Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act (COTPA), distribution is the primary route to consumer. The network includes pan shops, tobacco kiosks, general trade stores, and convenience outlets. ITC's Trade Marketing and Distribution (TM&D) vertical manages this network directly, without the reliance on third-party super-stockists or carrying-and-forwarding agents that most FMCG companies use. This direct relationship with retailers gives ITC unparalleled insight into sell-through data, competitive dynamics at the shelf level, and retail execution capability.

### FMCG-Others Distribution

ITC's most intelligent strategic decision in building the FMCG business was leveraging the cigarettes distribution network for its packaged foods and personal care launches. Aashirvaad, Sunfeast, Bingo!, and Yippee! were distributed through the same TM&D network that had spent decades servicing cigarettes — dramatically reducing the distribution costs that typically constrain a new FMCG entrant. This meant ITC could launch new FMCG products with day-one distribution in 600,000–800,000+ retail outlets, a capability that typically takes a decade and billions of rupees for a startup brand to build.

ITC sells through all trade channels: general trade (traditional kirana stores), modern trade (supermarkets, hypermarkets, convenience stores), and e-commerce (Amazon, Flipkart, Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart). The company has been investing in its direct-to-consumer digital capabilities, including its own platform ITC Store.

### Agribusiness Go-to-Market

ITC's Agribusiness operates a pure B2B model. On the procurement side, the e-Choupal network enables direct sourcing from farmers, bypassing the APMC (Agricultural Produce Market Committee) auction system for most commodities. On the sales side, leaf tobacco is exported directly to global leaf tobacco companies (Universal Corporation, Standard Industries, Alliance One International) and cigarette manufacturers. Wheat, spices, and coffee are sold to domestic food processors and international buyers. The segment benefits from ITC's established relationships and quality assurance systems built over decades.

### Hotels Distribution

ITC Hotels' revenues are generated through a combination of direct booking (ITC's own website and loyalty programme), online travel agents (OTAs such as MakeMyTrip, Booking.com), global distribution systems (GDS) for international travellers, and corporate contracts. The luxury segment is particularly dependent on direct relationships with corporates, travel management companies, and international travel networks. Fortune Hotels has a broader retail distribution approach given its mid-market positioning.

### ITC Infotech

ITC Infotech, the group's IT services subsidiary, operates a classic IT services go-to-market model targeting global mid-sized enterprises, with vertical expertise in consumer goods, retail, distribution, and manufacturing. The entity is headquartered in Bengaluru and has delivery centres and offices in the UK, USA, and other international locations.

## 6. INDUSTRY OVERVIEW

**ITC operates across five industries with fundamentally different growth profiles, regulatory environments, and competitive dynamics — requiring simultaneous mastery of tobacco economics, FMCG brand-building, luxury hospitality, industrial paper manufacturing, and commodity agriculture.**

### Indian FMCG Industry

The Indian Fast-Moving Consumer Goods market is one of the world's most attractive consumer arenas, with a current market size estimated at approximately ₹5.5–6 lakh crore (USD 65–72 billion). The market is characterised by its bifurcation between an organised sector (branded products) and a large unorganised sector, with the former steadily gaining share through formalization driven by GST implementation, digital payments, and growing consumer brand awareness.

Growth expectations for the organized FMCG sector stand at 9–13% per annum over the medium term, with premiumization in urban markets and volume growth in rural markets as the twin drivers. Urban India's consumption is driven by premiumization — consumers upgrading from commodity to branded, and from branded to premium branded. Rural India, which constitutes approximately 35–40% of FMCG revenues for most large players, is driven by income growth, aspirational consumption, and expanding retail reach.

The major bellwether companies — Hindustan Unilever (HUL, ~₹60,000 crore revenue), Nestle India, Britannia Industries, and Dabur India — provide the competitive reference framework. India's per-capita FMCG consumption remains at approximately 30–40% of China's per-capita level, implying a multi-decade runway for consumption growth as income levels converge.

### Cigarettes Industry

India's legal cigarettes market is among the most unique in the world: a heavily regulated, high-taxation oligopoly where the dominant player (ITC) has maintained approximately 75–77% volume share for decades, yet where volumes have been broadly flat to declining over the medium term due to aggressive excise taxation, health awareness, and structural competition from bidis (hand-rolled tobacco, taxed at only ₹1 per 1,000 sticks versus thousands of rupees per 1,000 sticks for cigarettes).

India's per-capita cigarette consumption is among the lowest in Asia — approximately 85–100 sticks per adult per year compared to 1,600+ in China and 600+ in Japan — largely because of the historic policy of taxing cigarettes at punitive levels while exempting bidis. This creates a paradox: total tobacco consumption in India is actually very high (bidis account for the majority of tobacco sticks consumed), but legal cigarettes are restricted by affordability to a relatively small segment of tobacco consumers.

The illicit cigarettes market — comprising smuggled international brands and counterfeit cigarettes — has been growing and accounts for approximately 26% of total cigarette consumption according to the Tobacco Institute of India. The Central Excise (Amendment) Act 2025, which dramatically increased specific excise duties effective January 2026, is expected to accelerate this trend, as the price differential between legal cigarettes and illicit alternatives widens.

ITC's response to volume pressure has been to offset it through premiumization: encouraging smokers to trade up to higher-priced brands (Gold Flake Kings, Classic) where revenue per stick is higher. This has broadly worked: cigarettes segment EBIT grew approximately 5% in FY25 despite modest volume growth.

### Hospitality Industry

India's hospitality industry has staged a strong post-COVID recovery, with RevPAR (Revenue per Available Room) across premium and luxury segments recovering to and exceeding pre-pandemic levels by FY24–25. The structural demand drivers are strong: India's large and growing business travel community (MICE demand), rapid expansion of domestic leisure tourism, wedding and events demand, and inbound international tourism growing from a low base.

The supply addition in the luxury segment has been deliberately slow, given the high capital intensity and long gestation periods for greenfield luxury hotel development. This supply constraint has been a tailwind for existing luxury inventory. ITC Hotels, with its LEED-certified luxury assets and ITC brand equity, is well-positioned in this structural demand-supply equilibrium.

The GST regime has been a mixed factor for hotels: room tariffs above ₹7,500/night attract 18% GST, which compresses realized yields at the margin but has been broadly absorbed by the industry through price hikes.

### Paper and Packaging Industry

India's paper and packaging industry is approximately ₹1.5 lakh crore in size, growing at 6–8% annually. E-commerce growth has been a powerful secular driver: the explosion of online retail creates structural demand for corrugated packaging, specialty papers, and value-added boards. Sustainability trends are pushing packaged goods companies to shift from plastic to paper-based packaging, a structural tailwind for ITC Bhadrachalam's value-added paperboards.

However, the near-term competitive headwind from low-priced Chinese and Indonesian paper imports has been severe, impressing margins significantly in FY25. Any resolution through anti-dumping duties would provide meaningful relief to the segment.

### Agribusiness and Leaf Tobacco Exports

India is one of the world's largest producers and exporters of leaf tobacco, with annual export values exceeding USD 1 billion. ITC is the dominant buyer and exporter in India, with deep relationships with global tobacco majors. The agri business also benefits from India's growing role as a food-exporting nation, with wheat, spices, coffee, and processed foods representing growing export opportunities.

Government policies — including MSP (Minimum Support Price) for tobacco and other crops — directly impact ITC's procurement economics. Any adverse shift in government policy toward tobacco cultivation could affect ITC's leaf tobacco supply chain.

### Regulatory Environment

The regulatory overlay across ITC's businesses is pervasive:
- **Tobacco taxation**: NCCD (National Calamity Contingent Duty) + specific excise duty + 28% GST, plus the new Health Security and National Security Cess framework proposed for 2026; total tax incidence on cigarettes is among the highest in the Asia-Pacific region
- **COTPA (Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act) restrictions**: Bans advertising of tobacco products across all media; mandates large pictorial health warnings on packaging; restricts sale near educational institutions
- **Plain packaging debate**: India has periodically debated plain packaging regulations (as implemented in Australia and the UK); implementation would further constrain ITC's brand differentiation in cigarettes
- **Hotel GST**: 18% GST on premium hotel rooms; a source of industry lobbying for rationalization
- **Agri regulations**: APMC rules, MSP policies, export restrictions on specific agricultural commodities

## 7. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

**ITC competes in distinct markets with entirely different competitive dynamics across its business segments; the conglomerate structure means that its competitors in cigarettes (a duopoly) are entirely different from its competitors in biscuits (a HUL-Britannia oligopoly) or luxury hotels (Taj, Oberoi).**

### Cigarettes: Dominant Oligopoly

ITC's competitive position in the Indian legal cigarettes market is one of the strongest franchises in Indian consumer goods. With approximately 75–77% market share by volume, ITC effectively sets the price in this market. Its two principal competitors:

**Godfrey Phillips India** (Philip Morris International affiliate): Holds approximately 10% market share and is the only credible challenger to ITC in the premium segment. Godfrey Phillips markets Four Square, Red & White, and Marlboro (under license). Its revenues stood at approximately ₹5,611 crore in FY25 (39.6% growth) — roughly one-sixth of ITC's cigarettes revenue. While Godfrey has been gaining share in some sub-segments, it lacks the manufacturing scale and distribution depth to threaten ITC's dominance.

**VST Industries** (formerly affiliated with BAT in certain markets): Holds approximately 9% market share, primarily in the south and east of India. VST's portfolio includes Charms, Charminar, and Prince. Its revenues of approximately ₹1,844 crore in FY25 make it a distant third. VST's margins are strong on a standalone basis but it has limited geographic and product breadth.

**Illicit/Bidi Market**: The structural competitor to legal cigarettes is the informal tobacco economy — bidis (hand-rolled tobacco at approximately one-tenth the price of cigarettes), smuggled international cigarettes, and counterfeit brands. The significant tax differential between bidis and cigarettes creates chronic downtrading risk for the legal cigarette industry.

**Overall assessment**: ITC's position in cigarettes constitutes a near-permanent moat — superior distribution, unmatched brand portfolio depth across price points, manufacturing scale advantages, and market share that has been durable through decades of regulatory pressure.

### FMCG-Others: Challenger Gaining Share in a Competitive Market

In the FMCG-Others segment, ITC competes against some of India's most formidable consumer goods companies:

**Hindustan Unilever Limited (HUL)**: India's dominant FMCG company with revenues of approximately ₹60,000 crore and market capitalisation of over ₹5 lakh crore. HUL's portfolio is concentrated in personal care (Dove, Lux, Surf Excel, Rin) and foods (Knorr soups, Kissan ketchup), with limited direct overlap with ITC's staples-heavy portfolio. HUL commands premium valuations (50x+ P/E) reflecting its execution track record and brand equity depth. In categories where they overlap (personal care, value foods), HUL's distribution and marketing resources pose a formidable competitive challenge.

**Nestle India**: Premium-positioned packaged foods with concentrated strength in Maggi noodles (direct competitor to ITC's Yippee!), KitKat and Munch confectionery, Nescafe coffee, and Milo dairy. Nestle's revenue is approximately ₹17,000–18,000 crore. The Maggi vs. Yippee! battle is one of India's most closely watched FMCG competitive dynamics; Yippee! has captured approximately 25–30% of the instant noodles market, primarily at Maggi's expense following the 2015 lead contamination recall (from which Maggi has largely recovered).

**Britannia Industries**: India's leading biscuits company with revenues of approximately ₹17,000 crore and strong profitability. Britannia's Good Day, Bourbon, 50-50, and Marie Gold brands compete directly with ITC's Sunfeast portfolio. Both companies have invested heavily in distribution and product innovation. Britannia has a stronger profitability track record in biscuits; ITC is gaining share through aggressive new product development.

**Tata Consumer Products**: The fastest-growing challenger in the FMCG space, having consolidated Tata's food and beverage assets. Tata Salt, Tata Tea, Tetley, and an expanding portfolio of adjacencies represent a competitive threat across multiple categories. Tata Consumer's revenue has been growing rapidly through both organic launches and acquisitions.

**Overall FMCG-Others assessment**: ITC is a credible challenger in most of its FMCG categories, with the distribution advantage as the key differentiator. The segment's EBIT profitability, while modest, is improving. Long-term success will depend on ITC's ability to build genuine brand equity that can sustain pricing power independent of distribution intensity.

### Hotels: Comparable to Taj, Below Oberoi in Ultra-Luxury

**Indian Hotels Company (Taj Hotels)**: The most directly comparable competitor to ITC Hotels in the luxury segment. Indian Hotels (part of the Tata Group) operates approximately 200+ properties under the Taj, SeleQtions, Vivanta, and Ginger brands. The Taj brand's heritage and international recognition (including New York and London properties) give it a slight edge in international MICE and tourism. ITC Hotels competes effectively with Taj in Indian luxury on sustainability, food, and location of specific flagship properties.

**EIH (Oberoi Hotels)**: Operates approximately 30 hotels in the ultra-luxury segment, commanding the highest room rates in India. Oberoi's smaller footprint but intense quality focus differentiates it from both ITC and Taj. ITC Hotels does not directly compete at the Oberoi price point in most markets.

**Overall Hotels assessment**: ITC Hotels is a Tier-1 luxury brand in India. The demerger allows it to be valued independently — with an enterprise value of approximately ₹25,000–28,000 crore post-listing — and to pursue an asset-light managed hotel growth strategy without the capital constraints of a conglomerate parent.

## 8. MARKET OPPORTUNITY

**The total addressable market for ITC's consumer businesses is among the largest in the world's fastest-growing large economy; the company's strategic positioning across the value chain from farmgate to consumer creates a durable competitive moat for capturing this opportunity.**

### Indian FMCG Total Addressable Market

The Indian FMCG market, estimated at ₹5.5–6 lakh crore (USD 65–72 billion), represents ITC's primary TAM. ITC's current addressable universe within FMCG — spanning staples, snacks, biscuits, noodles, personal care, and stationery — is estimated at approximately ₹2–2.5 lakh crore, representing approximately 35–40% of total market. At its current FMCG-Others revenue of approximately ₹22,000 crore, ITC has penetrated approximately 9–11% of its addressable TAM — a significant runway for growth.

India's per-capita FMCG consumption is approximately USD 50 per year compared to USD 120–140 in China — suggesting a 2.5x–3x consumption gap that will progressively close over the next decade as income levels converge. Demographic tailwinds are powerful: India's median age is approximately 28 years, the working-age population is expanding, urbanisation is accelerating, and first-generation consumers are actively upgrading from unbranded to branded products.

### Cigarettes: Value Growth Despite Volume Constraints

India's cigarette market presents a paradox of constrained volumes but potentially durable value growth. Per-capita cigarette consumption is approximately 85–100 sticks per adult per year — among the lowest in Asia despite high overall tobacco consumption. The low per-capita level reflects the price barrier created by decades of punitive excise taxation.

For ITC, the medium-term volume trajectory is modest at best — the January 2026 excise duty hike creates near-term volume pressure as legal cigarettes become significantly more expensive. However, ITC's pricing power and premiumization strategy mean that revenue and EBIT growth can continue to outpace volume growth. Even a scenario of flat volumes with 5–6% annual price increases implies mid-single-digit EBIT growth from the segment.

### FMCG-Others: Path to ₹30,000 Crore+ by FY28

ITC's management has articulated an aspiration to grow FMCG-Others revenues beyond ₹30,000 crore by FY28, implying a revenue CAGR of approximately 10–11% from the FY25 base of ₹22,000 crore. Key enablers: (1) Aashirvaad's continued share gains in branded staples as the formal food economy grows; (2) Sunfeast and Bingo! expanding into adjacent categories and geographies; (3) rapid growth of Yippee! and B Natural in beverages; (4) premium personal care under Fiama; and (5) international expansion of select brands into South Asian markets.

The segment's EBIT margin trajectory from approximately 9–10% in FY25 toward the 12–15% medium-term target would simultaneously drive revenue growth and profitability improvement, creating a meaningful earnings contribution even if never matching cigarettes' extraordinary margins.

### Agribusiness: Structural Beneficiary of India's Export Ambitions

India's ambition to double agricultural exports is a structural tailwind for ITC's Agri Business. India's leaf tobacco exports are already among the world's largest; wheat, spices, and coffee represent growing categories. The e-Choupal 4.0 platform, by deepening ITC's farmer engagement across 70,000 villages, will enhance procurement efficiency, reduce quality variability, and create a more resilient agricultural supply chain for ITC's food processing and export businesses.

## 9. RISK ASSESSMENT

**ITC's risk profile is shaped by four distinct categories: company-specific execution risks, industry structural risks, financial discipline risks, and macroeconomic sensitivities.**

### Company-Specific Risks

**Risk 1: Cigarette Volume Decline Accelerated by Excise Duty Hikes**

The Central Excise (Amendment) Act 2025, effective January 1, 2026, represents the most aggressive single-step tobacco taxation increase in India's post-liberalisation history, raising specific excise duties by 10x–15x. This creates a sharp price step-up for legal cigarettes that is likely to: (a) accelerate downtrading from legal cigarettes to bidis; (b) expand the illicit imported cigarettes market as the price arbitrage with smuggled brands widens; (c) reduce total industry volumes in the near term. The Tobacco Institute of India has explicitly warned of an illicit cigarette market surge. ITC's stock declined approximately 10% on announcement. The key uncertainty is the degree and permanence of volume loss — the historical evidence suggests that Indian smokers have been remarkably price-inelastic, but the scale of the current hike is without precedent. Management's ability to navigate through premiumization and pricing will be tested more severely than at any prior point.

**Risk 2: FMCG-Others Margin Compression from Brand Investment**

ITC has been investing approximately ₹1,500–2,000 crore annually in advertising and promotion for FMCG-Others brands, a level of investment required to compete against entrenched incumbents like HUL, Nestle, and Britannia. The segment's EBIT margins of approximately 9–10% remain below mature peers. Any acceleration in competitive intensity (particularly from Tata Consumer, which has been aggressively expanding) could require higher-than-planned marketing spending, compressing near-term profitability. The risk is that ITC's FMCG brands remain permanently dependent on promotional activity to drive volumes rather than achieving the brand equity that allows for independent pricing power.

**Risk 3: Conglomerate Discount Persistence**

Despite the Hotels demerger (which partially addressed the conglomerate discount), ITC continues to trade at a meaningful discount to a sum-of-parts valuation. The market assigns one blended multiple to a company whose segments deserved highly differentiated multiples: the cigarettes business deserves a premium oligopoly multiple; FMCG-Others deserves a growth FMCG multiple; Agribusiness is a commodity business. The conglomerate structure makes it difficult for sector-specific institutional investors to take concentrated positions. Further structural simplification — including a potential demerger or strategic review of the Paperboards segment — could be a value catalyst, but ITC's management has not signalled imminent steps beyond Hotels.

**Risk 4: BAT Strategic Overhang**

BAT's progressive reduction of its ITC stake — from ~29% to 22.9% post-May 2025 — introduces uncertainty around BAT's ultimate strategic intention. While BAT has stated this is motivated by capital allocation needs for its own buyback program (having raised approximately USD 3.5 billion from ITC stake sales in 2024–25), the overhang of a large shareholder with the ability and apparent willingness to sell creates periodic negative price pressure. If BAT were to sell below 20% (the typical threshold for strategic significance), it could signal a fundamental shift in the strategic relationship, potentially leading to questions about access to BAT's tobacco technology and global procurement networks.

### Industry/Market Risks

**Risk 5: Regulatory Tightening on Tobacco**

The Indian regulatory environment for tobacco has been consistently tightening: increasingly prominent pictorial health warnings (currently 85% of packaging area), advertising restrictions under COTPA, restrictions on display at point of sale, and the recurring proposals for plain packaging (as implemented in Australia, UK, and France). Each regulatory tightening constrains ITC's marketing flexibility in its highest-margin business. A shift to plain packaging would be particularly significant, as it would commoditize the brand equity that ITC has built in premium cigarettes brands like Gold Flake and Classic. Additionally, the proposed Health Security and National Security Cess on tobacco products under legislation being developed for 2026 could impose an additional layer of taxation beyond the already historic January 2026 excise hike.

**Risk 6: Competitive Intensity in FMCG-Others**

HUL, Nestle, Britannia, and Tata Consumer are all multi-decade FMCG businesses with deeply entrenched consumer relationships, distribution networks of comparable depth to ITC's, and aggressive investment capabilities. The competitive response to ITC's FMCG gains has been intensifying: Nestle has aggressively rebuilt Maggi's market share post-2015; Britannia has invested in distribution expansion to challenge ITC in Tier 2–4 cities; Tata Consumer is spending aggressively on building brand salience. ITC's challenger position, while strengthened by the distribution advantage, means that sustained market share gains will require continuous and significant brand investment, potentially at the cost of near-term EBIT margins.

**Risk 7: Agribusiness Commodity Price Volatility**

ITC's Agribusiness segment is exposed to global commodity price movements in leaf tobacco, wheat, spices, and coffee. Leaf tobacco prices are influenced by global supply-demand dynamics, disease cycles (blue mold, black shank), and the purchasing policies of major global tobacco companies. An unexpected decline in global leaf tobacco prices or a reduction in India's competitive positioning (due to currency movement or agronomic challenges) could significantly impact Agri Business revenues and profits. The segment's strong FY25 performance (25% revenue growth) was partly driven by favorable commodity prices and may not be sustainable at the same trajectory.

### Financial Risks

**Risk 8: Capital Allocation and Return on New Investments**

ITC's ₹20,000 crore medium-term manufacturing expansion plan announced at the FY25 AGM represents the company's most significant capital commitment in its non-cigarettes businesses. While the cigarettes business generates EBIT margins of 58–65%, the return profile of new FMCG manufacturing capacity will be fundamentally different — maturity EBIT margins for packaged foods are 12–18% at best. The aggregate ROCE of the ITC group will likely face dilution as more capital is deployed into FMCG fixed assets with lower marginal returns. The discipline question for management is whether the FMCG investment program eventually justifies its cost of capital — a question that will take several more years to answer definitively.

**Risk 9: Hotels Demerger Execution and Value Crystallisation**

The Hotels demerger, while successfully completed structurally, leaves open questions around value crystallisation for ITC shareholders. ITC Hotels Limited listed at approximately ₹188/share on BSE (January 29, 2025) and its market performance has been mixed, with the stock's trajectory partly dependent on Indian hospitality sector re-ratings and ITC Hotels' own growth execution. ITC's retained 40% stake means it remains exposed to ITC Hotels' performance but does not consolidate its earnings — creating a structural dividend dependency for value realisation. BAT has already sold most of its ITC Hotels shares (retaining ~6.3%), and continued institutional selling in the smaller-cap ITC Hotels stock remains a risk.

### Macroeconomic Risks

**Risk 10: Rural Demand Slowdown**

Approximately 35–40% of ITC's FMCG-Others revenues are linked to rural India's consumption demand. FY25 saw muted rural demand growth broadly across the Indian FMCG sector, with ITC's FMCG-Others revenue growth of approximately 5–7% below the 12–15% that bulls had anticipated. Structural rural demand depends on agricultural income, government transfer payments (PM Kisan, MGNREGS), normal monsoon patterns, and rural credit availability. An agricultural distress cycle — driven by poor monsoon, excessive rural food inflation, or delayed government payments — could significantly impact volume growth in ITC's core FMCG categories (atta, biscuits, snacks).

**Risk 11: INR Weakness and BAT Dividend Repatriation**

While ITC's business is primarily INR-denominated, there are two meaningful foreign exchange dimensions. First, the Agribusiness segment earns export revenues in USD and other hard currencies — INR weakness is actually a mild positive for this segment's competitiveness. Second, BAT receives dividends from ITC in INR and repatriates them in GBP, meaning a sharp INR depreciation affects the GBP value of BAT's ITC investment returns, potentially influencing BAT's holding period decision. For ITC itself, its modest USD-denominated import exposure (capital equipment, certain raw materials) means that sustained INR weakness would incrementally increase input costs. The net impact of INR weakness on ITC is modestly negative at the group level, though this risk is secondary to the fundamental operating risks.
