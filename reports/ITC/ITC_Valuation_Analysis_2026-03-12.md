## SECTION 1: VALUATION METHODOLOGY

### 1.1 Approach

We use a **three-pronged valuation framework**:

1. **Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) DCF** — Primary methodology (50% weight). ITC's multi-segment structure is best valued by individually discounting each division's cash flows, not by applying a single consolidated multiple. This eliminates the conglomerate discount by recognising each segment's true intrinsic value.

2. **EV/EBITDA Comparable Companies** — Secondary methodology (35% weight). We compare ITC against a carefully constructed peer set spanning (a) Indian FMCG companies, (b) global tobacco peers, and (c) Indian diversified consumer conglomerates. A blended multiple reflecting ITC's unique segment mix is applied.

3. **Dividend Discount Model (DDM)** — Supplementary methodology (15% weight). Given ITC's extraordinary 85–90% dividend payout ratio, cash generation visibility, and net-cash balance sheet, the DDM provides an important floor valuation.

**Price Target Derivation**: Weighted blend of the three approaches, rounded to the nearest ₹5.

## SECTION 4: DIVIDEND DISCOUNT MODEL (DDM)

ITC's exceptional dividend track record — 18 consecutive years of dividend increases, 85–90% payout ratio — makes the DDM a meaningful valuation check.

**Two-Stage Gordon Growth DDM:**
- Stage 1 (FY26E–FY30E): Growing dividends; DPS: ₹9.50 / ₹10.50 / ₹11.50 / ₹12.50 / ₹13.50
- Stage 2 (Terminal): DPS growing at 5.5% in perpetuity (conservative)
- Cost of Equity: 11.68%

**Stage 1 PV of Dividends (₹ per share)**:
- FY26E: 9.50 / 1.1168 = ₹8.51
- FY27E: 10.50 / 1.2472 = ₹8.42
- FY28E: 11.50 / 1.3924 = ₹8.26
- FY29E: 12.50 / 1.5549 = ₹8.04
- FY30E: 13.50 / 1.7363 = ₹7.78
- **Sum: ₹41.01**

**Stage 2 Terminal Value** (end FY30E):
- TV = DPS₃₁ / (Ke – g) = (13.50 × 1.055) / (0.1168 – 0.055) = 14.24 / 0.0618 = ₹230.4/share
- PV of TV = 230.4 / 1.7363 = ₹132.7/share

**DDM Intrinsic Value = ₹41.01 + ₹132.7 = ₹173.7/share**

*Note: DDM systematically undervalues ITC because the model only captures dividends, not the substantial retained earnings that build net cash (already at ₹28,000 Crore). The DDM floor of ~₹175 is NOT a ceiling — it illustrates the dividend-floor safety.*

### 6.2 Key Investment Catalysts (3–5 Catalysts)

**Catalyst 1: Excise Duty Absorption Confirmation (6–9 months)**
The market's primary fear is that the January 2026 excise hike will cause a structural collapse in cigarette volumes (implying illicit trade captures >10–15% of legal market share). ITC's Q1 FY27 (April–June 2026) and Q2 FY27 results will be the first full-quarter data points. We expect volumes to decline 3–5% (not 10%+), with ITC taking a price increase of 7–10% to preserve EBIT. Confirmation of this would be a significant positive catalyst, potentially re-rating the stock 15–20% from current levels. Historical parallels: post-FY10 and FY17 excise hikes, cigarette volumes recovered within 2–3 quarters.

**Catalyst 2: FMCG-Others Profitability Inflection (12 months)**
FMCG-Others crossed breakeven in FY23 after 15+ years of investment. With the segment now generating ~₹1,050 Crore EBIT on ₹21,980 Crore revenue (FY25A), the path to a 5–7% EBIT margin (₹2,500–3,500 Crore EBIT on projected ₹45,000+ Crore revenue) becomes highly visible as scale benefits accrue. Each 1% improvement in FMCG-Others EBIT margin adds approximately ₹450 Crore to consolidated EBIT (~2%). We model 7.0% by FY30E; market ascribes near-zero value to this segment.

**Catalyst 3: ITC Hotels Demerger Value Crystallisation (6–12 months)**
ITC Hotels Limited listed on January 29, 2026. ITC retains a 40% stake. As the Hotels company executes its expansion roadmap (70 new properties by 2028, targeting 200+ total), the market will begin ascribing increasing value to ITC's 40% stake (~₹8,500 Crore by our estimate, vs. potentially ₹12,000–15,000 Crore as Hotels re-rates on standalone listing premium). Each ₹10,000 Crore increase in Hotels stake value adds ~₹8/share to ITC.

**Catalyst 4: Capital Allocation — Buyback / Special Dividend (12–18 months)**
ITC's net cash position has grown to ₹28,000 Crore (vs. ₹16,000 Crore in FY21) — equivalent to ~7% of current market cap. With Hotels demerged and FMCG capex requirements moderating, we believe ITC will execute either (a) a special dividend, (b) a buyback, or (c) accretive bolt-on FMCG acquisitions. A ₹10,000 Crore buyback at ₹307 would retire ~3% of shares and add ₹0.50 to FY27E EPS. This would signal management confidence at the current depressed valuation.

**Catalyst 5: BAT Stake Resolution (12–24 months)**
BAT reduced its ITC stake from ~29% to 22.9% via a £2.2 billion block trade in May 2025. While this created an overhang, BAT's continued 22.9% ownership has created a structural floor. A further stake reduction (to 15–20%) or a clear commitment to maintain holding would remove uncertainty. Either resolution is incrementally positive: a further sale at current prices is likely dilutive to BAT's own financials and unlikely; a hold commitment removes the overhang.

### 6.3 Key Risks to Price Target

**Risk 1: Sustained Cigarette Volume Decline > 4% p.a. (HIGH IMPACT)**
If the January 2026 excise hike triggers a structural shift toward illicit/smuggled cigarettes (which has happened in countries like Australia), ITC's cigarette EBIT could decline 15–20% versus our projections. Our model assumes only a 2–3% volume CAGR decline — if this proves to be 4–5%, our price target would fall to ₹280–300. Mitigation: ITC's cigarette business has survived 30+ years of excise hikes; the domestic illicit market is structurally constrained by distribution networks.

**Risk 2: Further Tobacco Regulatory Tightening (MEDIUM IMPACT)**
Union Budget 2027 could include another excise hike or the introduction of plain packaging regulations (proposed but not yet enacted). GST Council has also been evaluating moving cigarettes to a higher GST slab. These regulatory risks are real but hard to time. Our bear case embeds one more excise hike in FY28E.

**Risk 3: FMCG-Others Competitive Intensity (MEDIUM IMPACT)**
HUL, Nestle, and Britannia have demonstrated their willingness to invest aggressively to protect market share in their respective categories. Aashirvaad (atta), Sunfeast (biscuits), and Yippee! (noodles) each face entrenched category leaders. ITC's FMCG margin expansion thesis depends on sales leverage — if competitive intensity forces higher A&P spend, margin expansion could be slower than modelled. Risk: FMCG-Others remains at 3–4% EBIT margin through FY30E (vs. our 7.0% forecast), reducing consolidated EBIT by ₹1,200–2,000 Crore.

**Risk 4: Agribusiness Commodity Volatility (LOW IMPACT)**
Leaf tobacco export revenue (~₹13,000 Crore) is dependent on global crop cycles, geopolitical export demand (India is a major exporter to US, EU, and SE Asia), and INR/USD dynamics. A 10% deterioration in realizations could impact EBIT by ₹400–500 Crore. This is manageable relative to consolidated EBIT of ₹24,000+ Crore.

**Risk 5: Conglomerate Discount Persists (LOW PROBABILITY, HIGH IMPACT IF MATERIALISED)**
If ITC fails to demonstrate clear strategic value from its multi-segment structure — particularly if FMCG-Others does not achieve profitability inflection — institutional investors may persistently apply a conglomerate discount, capping valuation at 12–15x EV/EBITDA. This scenario implies the stock could trade sideways at ₹300–320 for an extended period even with improving fundamentals.
